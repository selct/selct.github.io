MECHATRONIC MUSIC

SELCT is a world leader in mechatronic music and its sub-disciplines of robotic music and kinetic sound sculpture. A core focus of SELCT is the integration of mechatronic engineering practices into the development of these instruments, allowing for enhanced accuracy, precision, and performance; such enhanced capabilities result in more compelling affordances for composers and musicians. Indeed, SELCT emphasises not only the development of mechatronic instruments but also the performative and installation use of them: through SELCT, Wellington has become a hotbed of mechatronic music performance and composition.

SELCT’s current research on mechatronic music focuses on the means by which musicians may interface with new expressive instruments. In addition to developing, characterising, and using these instruments, new human/robot interfaces are being explored and tested.

SELCT is actively recruiting postgraduate students (at Master's and Doctoral level) for research projects centred on the study of mechatronic music. Additionally, SELCT seeks collaborators and performers from other institutions for development, performance, and installation use of mechatronic instruments.

APPLIED MIR

SELCT has a strong presence in the field of music information retrieval for pedagogy and computational ethnomusicology. Many of SELCT’s contributions to these fields come in the form of multimodal systems: using a variety of sensing techniques to obtain datasets of higher dimensionality than is traditionally available. SELCT researchers often feed this multimodal data acquisition and feature extraction approach into machine learning frameworks, using these data in practical classification and clustering applications.

A recent example of an applied MIR application explored by SELCT is the development of a gesture recognition system used to acquire and preserve expert-level musicians’ instrument-playing techniques. Similarly, multimodal data acquisition approaches have been used for percussion instrument pedagogy. Additionally, SELCT researchers have utilised MIR features for kinetic sound art installation purposes.

SELCT is actively seeking postgraduate students who wish to engage in research projects that utilise music information retrieval and machine learning approaches for musicological purposes, pedagogy, and for creative performance and installation use. For more information, please contact us.

NEW MUSICAL INTERFACES

SELCT faculty and students are innovators in the field of new interfaces for musical expression. As innovators in this field, SELCT researchers pursue new means by which sensors and data acquisition systems may be used to control and effect audiovisual experiences. Notable recent projects exploring these new interfaces include the development of a glove-style interface that utilises cutting-edge inertial measurement units. This unit is used both for performance as well as for musicological outputs; it is currently being explored as an intuitive way of controlling parametrically-dense mechatronic instruments. Similarly, new sensor apparatuses are being applied to traditional instruments to allow our researchers to increase their understanding of performers’ playing styles.

In addition to creating tools for musicological research, SELCT members are active developers of new interfaces (software-based as well as hardware-based) to be used in real-world performances. Notable recent applications include the development of hardware and software tools to position sounds within the listening space.

SELCT is an excellent place for those interested in studying and developing new musical interfaces. Learn more about joining us here.

SOUND ART AND INSTALLATION

In addition to developing mechatronic musical instruments, MIR systems, and new musical interfaces, SELCT artists actively engage in the creation of a diverse range of types of sound art. These artworks range from kinetic and mechanical devices through to art-oriented games, app, and mixed-media compositions.

As part of the development of this artwork, SELCT researchers continue to engage in historical and critical studies of sound art, sonic arts, and music; a number of refereed journal and conference publications reflect this engagement into the study of prior works and their effect on the current state of sound art. In addition to the development of writings about the field of sound art, SELCT members actively install and perform new works.

Notable recent avenues of sound art study include an examination of Brutalist architecture and its translation to mechatronic sound sculpture, as well as an ongoing study of artworks that explore psychoacoustic phenomena and the means by which listeners perceive sonic elements. SELCT faculty and students are closely engaged with the artmaking process, showing works internationally in festivals and performances; for prospective students interested in pursuing sound art-based studies, please contact us.
